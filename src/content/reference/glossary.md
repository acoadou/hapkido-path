This glossary explains practical training terms used in technique notes. Definitions are intentionally short so they can support practice without becoming a separate theory manual.

## Roles and Situations

### Attacker

The partner who initiates the grab, strike, push, or other problem the technique is meant to address.

### Defender

The partner practicing the response. In cooperative training, the defender should still protect the attacker while applying the technique.

## Grips and Attacks

### Grip

Any hold on the body, clothing, or limb that gives the attacker contact and a way to apply force.

### Sleeve grab

A grip on the sleeve or lower arm area, commonly used to study wrist movement, posture, and entry angles.

### Outside sleeve

A sleeve or wrist-area grip made from the outside line of the defender's arm, rather than across the centerline.

### Cross-hand grab

A grip where the attacker's right hand holds the defender's right side, or the left hand holds the defender's left side, crossing the attacker's body line.

## Locks and Controls

### Wrist lock

A control that directs the wrist through a limited range so the attacker must move, release, or lose posture.

### Arm bar

A straight-arm control that uses alignment through the wrist, elbow, and shoulder to limit bending and movement.

### Shoulder control

Using the arm, elbow line, or body position to influence the shoulder so the attacker cannot freely turn or recover balance.

### Pressure

Steady force applied through structure and direction. Good pressure guides the partner; excessive pressure creates unnecessary risk.

## Throws, Takedowns, and Pins

### Takedown

A method for bringing the attacker to the ground while maintaining enough control to protect both partners.

### Pin

A hold or position that limits the attacker's ability to stand, turn, or continue attacking after reaching the ground.

### Roll

A falling skill that spreads impact through a curved path so the body can land and recover more safely.

## Body Mechanics

### Balance breaking

Disrupting the attacker's posture so they can no longer apply force efficiently or recover their position easily.

### Centerline

An imagined line through the body's middle that helps explain direct pressure, openings, and safe positioning.

### Posture

The body's organized alignment for standing, moving, and applying force without unnecessary tension.

### Leverage

Using position, angle, and distance so a smaller movement can affect a larger or stronger structure.
