These principles are broad study notes for cooperative Hapkido practice. Exact etiquette, terminology, and teaching emphasis may vary by instructor or school.

## Safety First

Train at a speed and intensity that both partners can manage. Clear practice is more useful than forcing a technique before either partner understands the movement.

## Tap Early

Tap as soon as a lock, pin, or stretch becomes unsafe or unclear. Tapping is communication, not failure, and it helps both partners keep learning.

## Apply Locks Slowly

Joint locks should build gradually. Move slowly enough for the attacker to feel the direction and for the defender to stop before pain or injury occurs.

## Protect Your Partner's Joints

Avoid sudden twisting, bouncing pressure, or competing strength against a trapped joint. Keep control of the limb and release immediately when your partner taps or asks you to stop.

## Relaxation

Unnecessary tension makes movement slower and easier to resist. Relaxation does not mean weakness; it means using only the effort needed for the task.

## Circular Motion

Many Hapkido movements use arcs instead of straight pushes. Circular motion can redirect force, change the angle of a grip, and make transitions smoother.

## Use Structure Instead of Strength

Align your stance, hands, elbows, shoulders, and hips so your body supports the technique. Good structure reduces the need to pull or muscle through resistance.

## Move the Body With the Lock

Do not isolate the hands from the rest of the body. Step, turn, or drop your weight so the lock is connected to whole-body movement.

## Break Posture Before Forcing a Technique

A technique is safer and clearer when the attacker is already off balance. If the attacker remains upright and strong, adjust angle, distance, or timing before adding force.

## Control the Hand, Elbow, and Shoulder

The hand may start the lock, but the elbow and shoulder often decide whether the attacker can turn out, strike, or regain posture. Watch the whole arm, not just the wrist.

## Partner Etiquette

Work at the level your partner can safely receive. Give honest but cooperative energy, reset without argument, and ask questions when a movement or level of contact is unclear.

## School Differences

Schools may use different commands, bowing routines, teaching progressions, and preferred explanations. Follow your instructor's directions in class and use these notes as general support material.
