## Summary

Sleeve 5-6 is a combined reference for two closely related school variations until their differences are documented clearly.

This page is detailed for study and review. Practice slowly. Joint locks and takedowns can injure the wrist, elbow, shoulder, neck, or back if applied suddenly or resisted. Use qualified instruction, communicate with your partner, and stop immediately on sharp pain.

## Starting position

Uke has established a sleeve grab and maintains the grip long enough for study. Nage stands balanced, relaxed, and close enough to move around the gripping hand without yanking backward.

Include these details when class notes are added:

- Which hand attacks.
- Which sleeve or arm is grabbed.
- Whether the grip is same-side or cross-side.
- Whether nage starts square, angled, relaxed, or already moving.

## Goal

- Expose or control the grabbing hand.
- Move around the grip instead of pulling straight back.
- Connect the wrist, elbow, shoulder, and posture into one line of control.
- Break posture before asking uke to fall or receive a pin.
- Transition to the ending control used by the current school for Sleeve 5-6.

## Entry condition

This technique makes sense when uke keeps the sleeve grip and nage has enough freedom to turn, step, and connect to the gripping hand. If uke releases early, the technique becomes a release or reset instead of a lock or takedown.

## Step-by-step

### Step 1 — Establish the line of the grip

This draft page reserves the technique sequence for verified class notes. The movement should be entered slowly, with the defender moving around the grip and checking that uke can follow safely.

Check that the shoulders stay down, the elbow is not locked rigidly, and the movement is powered by the whole body rather than the hand alone.

### Step 2 — Capture and align the hand

Use the free hand to secure the gripping hand when the position is exposed. Keep the captured hand near the centerline so the defender does not create a long, weak lever.

The exact grip shape should be filled in from verified notes. Record palm direction, thumb position, and whether the hands sandwich, fold, or rotate uke's wrist.

### Step 3 — Rotate through the wrist and posture

Apply the wrist rotation gradually. The pressure should travel through uke's wrist into the elbow and shoulder so posture changes before pain becomes sharp.

Do not twist only with the fingers. Turn the torso, settle the weight, and keep the elbows connected to the body.

### Step 4 — Finish with control

Complete the school finish for Sleeve 5-6. If the finish includes a takedown, uke should already be off balance and should have enough space and falling skill. If the finish is a pin, maintain pressure gradually and allow clear tapping.

## Ending position

The ending position should record:

- Uke's orientation.
- Nage's stance or kneeling position.
- Which joints are controlled.
- Whether nage remains standing or follows uke down.
- How pressure is maintained without sudden cranking.

## Short sequence

Sleeve 5-6 start → sleeve grip recognized → circular or angled entry → gripping hand controlled → wrist and elbow line affected → posture breaks → school finish or pin.

## Key mechanics

- Do not yank the sleeve straight back.
- Keep the captured hand close to the centerline.
- Use hip turn, shoulder connection, and body drop instead of arm strength only.
- Break posture before increasing joint pressure.
- Keep enough slack in the body to feel uke's movement, but not so much that structure collapses.

## Footwork

Document the exact step pattern after class verification. For now, practice the likely pattern as slow footwork only:

- Step off the direct line of force.
- Turn the hips with the arm path.
- Keep the feet under the body rather than reaching too far.
- Finish in a stable stance before adding pressure.

Common wrong footwork is stepping away from the grip while leaving the arm behind, which creates a tug-of-war and weakens the lock.

## Hand and arm details

Record the exact hand placement after instructor review. The study priority is to keep nage's elbows connected and to avoid forcing uke's wrist faster than uke's elbow and shoulder can follow.

## Effect on attacker

Uke should feel posture change through the structure, not a sudden isolated attack on the wrist. The wrist angle should begin to turn the elbow and shoulder, causing uke to step, fold, or descend safely.

## What uke should feel

- Pressure increases gradually through the wrist.
- The shoulder begins to follow the wrist before pain becomes sharp.
- Balance changes clearly enough that uke can move with the lock.
- Uke can tap early or step out when the pressure is unclear.

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | Why it fails | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Pulling straight back | Creates a strength contest against the grip | Move around the grip in a circle or angle |
| Holding the hand far from the body | Weak leverage and poor connection | Keep control near centerline |
| Twisting only with the fingers | Isolates the wrist and can injure uke | Connect wrist rotation to elbow, shoulder, torso, and footwork |
| Rushing the takedown | Uke falls before structure is ready | Break posture first, then finish |
| Ignoring uke's feedback | Turns study into unsafe resistance | Communicate and stop on sharp pain |

## Safety notes

- Apply wrist pressure gradually.
- Do not crank the wrist suddenly.
- Uke should move with the pressure rather than resist suddenly.
- Stop immediately on sharp wrist, elbow, or shoulder pain.
- Practice takedowns only with adequate falling skill, space, and instructor approval.

## Solo practice

Solo practice can train the arm path, hip turn, step-pivot pattern, and level change. The lock and takedown cannot be fully learned solo because timing, grip pressure, and uke's structure require a partner.

## Partner drill

Start from the sleeve grip. Uke gives a realistic but cooperative hold. Nage performs the entry slowly, pauses when the hand is controlled, then adds wrist rotation only until uke's posture changes. Uke moves with the pressure and taps early.
